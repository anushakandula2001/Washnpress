PART A — DETAILED PROJECT REPORT
1. Executive Summary
Wash N Press is a hyperlocal, subscription-based laundry platform serving gated communities,
apartment societies, and PG/co-living accommodations. The model combines app-based
convenience for residents with a decentralized, community-embedded operating structure: each
serviced society's micro-laundry unit is owned and run by a two-member women's team
recruited from the local community, supported by Wash N Press through equipment, training,
and working-capital assistance.
The project is built around three pillars: convenience (predictable subscription-based laundry
service with live tracking), livelihood creation (women's micro-entrepreneurship at the
community level), and sustainability (low-water and water-recycling washing machinery to
reduce the environmental footprint of a traditionally water-intensive service).
2. Project Background & Rationale
Urban housing societies in India represent a dense, underserved market for recurring household
services. Laundry remains a daily time burden for residents, while existing solutions are either
informal (unreliable, no quality assurance) or centralized commercial laundromats (logistics-
heavy, impersonal). Wash N Press addresses this gap by embedding the service physically
inside the community it serves.
Simultaneously, urban and peri-urban women — particularly those with limited access to formal
employment due to caregiving responsibilities, mobility constraints, or lack of formal
qualifications — represent an underutilized workforce that benefits from hyperlocal, flexible
micro-enterprise opportunities within walking distance of home. This project is structured to
address both gaps with one operating model.
3. Project Objectives
• Deliver a reliable, app-based laundry subscription service to residents of partnered
housing societies.
• Establish a women-led micro-enterprise (two-member unit) in each serviced society,
providing equipment, training, and initial working-capital support.
• Reduce fresh-water consumption per garment processed through water-saving and
water-recycling machinery.
• Build a scalable, replicable operating and franchise-style model that can expand society-
by-society and city-by-city without proportional increases in central overhead.
• Create a measurable social and environmental impact track record suitable for impact-
investment, CSR partnership, and government MSME/SHG scheme alignment.
4. Social Impact Model — Women-Led Micro-Units

Each society-level micro-laundry unit is operated by a two-member women's team, ideally
residents of that society or its immediate vicinity, minimizing commute and enabling flexible
hours around family responsibilities. Wash N Press functions as the enabling platform: providing
the machinery (capex), the app/ops system, training, and an initial operating-capital cushion
during ramp-up; the women's team provides labor, day-to-day unit management, and the
community trust relationship that's difficult for a centralized vendor to replicate.
4.2 Recruitment, Training & Support
1. Identify and recruit candidates through local women's Self-Help Groups (SHGs), RWA
networks, or direct community outreach — prioritizing residents of the target society.
2. Provide a structured training program covering machine operation, fabric handling, the
Ops App workflow, basic customer service, and hygiene/safety standards.
3. Provide initial working-capital support (e.g., a guaranteed minimum monthly draw for the
first 3–6 months) to de-risk the ramp-up period before subscriber volume stabilizes.
4. Pair each new unit with an experienced mentor unit (once the network has matured) for
peer support.
4.3 Revenue-Sharing Model
A blended structure is recommended over a flat wage, to align incentives with service quality
and retention: a fixed monthly base draw per unit (covering baseline livelihood needs) plus a
percentage share of subscription and add-on revenue above an agreed volume threshold. This
mirrors structures used in SHG-linked micro-enterprise and managed-franchise models, and
should be finalized in consultation with a legal/financial advisor alongside applicable schemes
such as India's National Rural Livelihood Mission (NRLM/DAY) or state-level women's
enterprise support programs, where the unit may qualify for additional subsidy or low-interest
credit support.
4.4 Governance & Accountability
• Each unit operates under a written partnership agreement defining equipment
ownership, revenue share, quality standards, and renewal/exit terms.
• Unit performance (quality scores, complaint rate, turnaround time) is tracked through the
same Ops App and Admin Console used for centralized hubs, ensuring consistent
service standards across the network.
• A grievance and support channel is maintained for the women's teams themselves,
separate from the resident-facing support system.
5. Sustainability Model — Water Conservation
5.1 Rationale
Laundry is among the more water-intensive household services, and centralizing or
decentralizing that water use without addressing efficiency simply relocates the environmental
cost rather than reducing it. Building water efficiency into the core machinery choice — rather
than treating it as an optional upgrade — makes sustainability a structural feature of the
business rather than a marketing claim.

5.2 Machinery Approach
• Use commercial washing machines with built-in greywater recycling/reuse modules,
which capture and filter rinse-cycle water for reuse in subsequent wash cycles, reducing
fresh-water draw per load.
• Evaluate low-water-consumption machine models (front-load, high-efficiency commercial
units) over conventional top-load machines for all new unit installations.
• Where feasible at higher-volume hubs, consider on-site greywater treatment for reuse in
non-potable applications (e.g., facility cleaning), subject to local water-authority approval.
5.3 Economic Framing
Water-efficient machinery should be evaluated as a capex decision with a defined payback
period — higher upfront equipment cost weighed against monthly water (and associated
heating/pumping) savings — rather than imposed uniformly regardless of unit economics. High-
volume hubs, where water cost is a larger share of operating expense, are the natural first
deployment point; the payback period should improve as machine costs fall with scale,
informing the rollout sequence to smaller micro-units.
5.4 Impact Tracking
• Track and report liters of fresh water saved per garment / per unit / network-wide, as a
core sustainability KPI alongside financial metrics.
• Use this data point in RWA pitches, CSR partnership conversations, and impact-investor
reporting.

9. Expected Social & Environmental Outcomes
• Direct livelihood creation for two women per serviced society, scaling linearly with
society count.

centralized, anonymous vendor relationship.

schemes, and impact-focused investors.

13. Operations App — Functional Requirements
13.1 Women's-Unit Operator Workflow (Primary Model)
8. Operator logs in and views the day's bookings for their society (no multi-society routing
needed — single-society scope).
9. At pickup (in-society, short-distance), operator logs garment count/category and applies
QR batch tag.
10. Garments processed on-site: Wash → Dry → Iron → QC, each stage logged in-app.
11. QC fail/damage triggers automatic resident notification and support ticket — never
silently proceeds.
12. Operator scans QR at delivery to confirm match against pickup manifest; captures
delivery confirmation (OTP/signature).
13. App logs water usage per cycle (where machinery supports automated reporting) or
allows manual log entry, feeding the sustainability dashboard.
13.2 Hub & Route Model (High-Density Clusters Only)
• Used only where a society/cluster has outgrown micro-unit capacity; retains the route-
batching and agent-delivery workflow from the original hub model.
• Same QR-tagging, QC-checkpoint, and discrepancy-blocking logic applies regardless of
operating model.
13.3 Inventory & Garment Tracking
• QR (Phase 1) or RFID (Phase 2, pending pilot loss-rate data) batch tag is the system of
record from pickup through delivery.
• Lost/damaged garment reporting workflow with photo evidence, linked to order and
resident for compensation.
13.4 Women's-Unit Operations Support
• In-app access to training materials and refresher modules.

17. End-to-End Order Workflow (Summary)
17. Resident books or has a recurring pickup slot with their society's women's unit.
18. Operator logs garment count, applies QR batch tag, marks pickup complete → resident
notified.
19. On-site processing: Wash → Dry → Iron → QC, with status updates at each stage;
water usage logged.
20. QC pass moves to "Ready for Delivery"; QC fail triggers resident notification and support
ticket.
21. Operator delivers, scans QR to confirm match, captures delivery confirmation.
22. Order closed; garment usage deducted from subscription cap; resident may rate and
dispute within a configurable window.
